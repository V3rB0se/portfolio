Evolution and Ecology of Spatial Coevolutionary Genetic Algorithms 

by Yutaro Shimizu

Despite the very digital impression of computational machines today, inventors of computers were in fact very motivated to recreate life inside an artificial processor. A mathematician who took on this task was John Holland who first introduced the blueprint of Genetic Algorithms (Adaptation in natural and artificial systems, 1975). Since its invention, GA has been very successful in both areas of research and application across domains of fields. Several successors took on the artificial life route to produce nature-inspired optimizers that were capable of solving digit sorting and function induction (Hillis 1991, Williams and Mitchell 2006). I attempted to extend their work, by applying Thompson’s Geogrpahic Mosaic Theory of Coevolution to the mechanics of GA. A good analogy can be extremely effective at generating creative ideas and solving problems, but the temptation to identify superficial similarities can get in the way. Good analogy requires a grasp of the key connections between the two systems, with respect to the purpose of making the analogies. We practiced good analogies, and crowdsourced strategies to mitigate superficial analogies that can be applied outside of research settings by drawing analogies from other majors to improve the digit classifier’s performance.
